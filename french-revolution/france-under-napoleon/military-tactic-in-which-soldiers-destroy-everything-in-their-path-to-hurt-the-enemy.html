Military tactic in which soldiers destroy everything in their path to hurt the enemy.

(A) Guerilla Warfare

(B) Sans-Culotte

(C) ** Scorched Earth Policy

(D) Plebiscite

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -A scorched-earth policy is a military strategy that aims to destroy anything that might be useful to the enemy. Any assets that could be used by the enemy may be targeted, which usually includes obvious weapons, transport vehicles, communication sites, and industrial resources.

Concept note-2: -A notable example of the use of this tactic came during the American civil war in 1864, when Union General William Tecumseh Sherman and his soldiers burnt everything in sight as they marched through Confederate areas.

Concept note-3: -The fleeing Russian forces adopted a “scorched earth” strategy, seizing or burning any supplies that the French might pillage from the countryside.

Concept note-4: -After the German invasion of the Soviet Union in June 1941, Soviet soldiers followed a “scorched earth” policy to hinder the German advance. In this German newsreel footage, German soldiers approach a burning village, one of many destroyed during the invasion of the Soviet Union.

Concept note-5: -Part of Russia’s eventual success in the war was the so-called scorched-earth tactics, which consisted of burning Moscow to the ground so that its enemy could use neither the resources nor property in place.
